Module I

Introduction to Ethical Hacking

Can Hacking Be Ethical?

The noun “hacker” describes a person who enjoys learning the details of computer systems and stretching their capabilities. The verb “hacking” can mean rapidly developing new programs or reverse-engineering existing software to improve its code, performance, or efficiency.

Terminology

The term cracker is often used for someone who applies hacking skills offensively. An ethical hacker is a security professional who uses those same skills for defensive and protective purposes.

  • Threat – An action or event that might prejudice security; a potential violation.
  • Vulnerability – A weakness (design or implementation) that can lead to an unexpected, undesirable event.
  • Target of Evaluation – An IT system/product/component chosen for security assessment.
  • Attack – An assault on system security originating from an intelligent threat actor.
  • Exploit – A defined method to breach an IT system through a vulnerability.

Elements of Security

Security is the state where the risk of undetected theft, tampering, or disruption of information and services is kept low. Any hacking event will impact one or more essential security elements.

  • Confidentiality — Concealment of information or resources.
  • Authenticity — Assurance of the origin/identity of information.
  • Integrity — Trustworthiness of data; protection against unauthorized modification.
  • Availability — The ability to access information or resources when needed.

What Does a Malicious Hacker Do?

Phase 1 - Reconnaissance

Reconnaissance refers to the preparatory phase where an attacker seeks to gather as much information as possible about a target of evaluation prior to launching an attack. It can involve network scanning (external or internal) without authorization.

Business Risk – ‘Notable’ – Generally noted as "rattling the door knobs" to see if someone is watching and responding. It could indicate a future point of return when more is known about the target.

Passive reconnaissance involves monitoring network data for patterns and clues (e.g., sniffing, information gathering).

Active reconnaissance involves probing the network to detect:

  • accessible hosts
  • open ports
  • location of routers
  • details of operating systems and services

Phase 2 - Scanning

Scanning refers to the pre-attack phase when the hacker scans the network using information gathered during reconnaissance.

Business Risk – ‘High’ – Scanning often finds a single point of entry and can reveal exploitable vulnerabilities.

Techniques may include:

  • dialers
  • port scanners
  • network mapping and sweeping
  • vulnerability scanners

Phase 3 - Gaining Access

Gaining Access is the actual attack phase when the hacker exploits a vulnerability to enter the system. Exploits can occur over LAN, locally, via the Internet, offline, or through deception/theft.

Examples: stack-based buffer overflows, denial of service, session hijacking, password filtering, etc.

Influencing factors: system architecture and configuration, attacker skill level, and initial access obtained.

Business Risk – ‘Highest’ – Access can compromise OS, application, or network layers.

Phase 4 - Maintaining Access

Maintaining Access refers to efforts by the hacker to retain control of an exploited system. Attackers may harden their access against others and install mechanisms to return later.

Common tools: Backdoors, rootkits, Trojans and Trojan horse backdoors. Attackers can upload, download or manipulate data, apps, and configurations.

Phase 5 - Covering Tracks

Covering Tracks includes activities to avoid detection and prolong misuse of the system — removing evidence or hiding presence. Reasons include prolonged stay, continued use of resources, avoiding legal action, or preparing new attacks.

Techniques: steganography, tunneling, altering log files, and other log manipulation.

Attackers might stay undetected for long periods, or use this phase to begin fresh reconnaissance on related targets.

What Do Ethical Hackers Do?

Quote: “If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles.” — Sun Tzu

Overview Phases Skill Profile

Ethical Hacker — Goals & Questions

Ethical hackers try to answer the following important questions about a target system:

  • What can the intruder see on the target system? (Reconnaissance & Scanning)
  • What can an intruder do with that information? (Gaining & Maintaining Access)
  • Does anyone at the target notice the intruder's attempts or success? (Detection & Covering Tracks)

When hired, an ethical hacker first asks the organization what it protects, from whom, and how much resource it will commit to protection. That helps scope tests and prioritize fixes.

Typical Phases (High-level)

Reconnaissance

Collect public & semi-public information (DNS, WHOIS, website, social media, open ports).

Scanning & Enumeration

Identify services, versions and misconfigurations the target exposes.

Exploitation / Gaining Access

Use weaknesses to obtain access — always within agreed scope and rules of engagement.

Maintaining Access & Covering Tracks

If in scope, ethically demonstrate persistence and assess detection/forensics gaps. Then remove traces per contract.

Skill Profile of an Ethical Hacker

  • Strong computer fundamentals and technical expertise across domains.
  • In-depth knowledge of target platforms (Windows, Unix, Linux).
  • Excellent networking knowledge — protocols, stacks, and related hardware/software.
  • Good understanding of security principles and incident response; not always a dedicated security professional but often works alongside them.

Best Practices (Ethical & Legal)

  1. Always have written authorization (scope, time, and rules).
  2. Report findings clearly and provide remediation steps.
  3. Keep client data safe — do not exfiltrate or leak sensitive information.
  4. Coordinate with defenders to minimize operational impact.

Security Evaluation — Three Components

Any security evaluation involves three components. Below each phase is described and presented in a bordered card.

Preparation

In this phase, a formal contract is signed that contains a non-disclosure clause as well as a legal clause to protect the ethical hacker against any prosecution that may arise during the conduct phase. The contract also outlines the infrastructure perimeter, allowed evaluation activities, time schedules, and resources available to the tester.

Conduct

During the conduct phase the tester performs agreed-upon tests and documents findings. The evaluation technical report is prepared based on discovered vulnerabilities, evidence collected, and impact analysis. Tests are performed only within the agreed perimeter and timeframe.

Conclusion

In the conclusion phase results and recommendations are communicated to the organization or sponsors. Corrective advice and remediation steps are suggested, prioritized by risk, and follow-up activities are scheduled if required.

Modes of Ethical Hacking

Remote network

An ethical hacker pretends to be an attacker on the Internet and checks a company's websites or servers for weak spots.

Example: Scanning a school website to find a page that anyone can break into.

Remote dial-up network

Tests old phone/modem access to see if someone can call in and get into systems.

Example: Trying default modem passwords on an old office line to see if it gives access.

Local network

Acts like someone already inside the school or office network who tries to see secrets or move to other computers.

Example: Plugging into a classroom network port and trying to read files on other computers.

Stolen equipment

Checks what happens if a laptop or USB stick is lost or stolen — can the finder see private data?

Example: Finding an unencrypted laptop and opening student records without a password.

Social engineering

Tests how people behave: the hacker tricks employees into giving passwords or doing risky things.

Example: Sending a fake email that asks a teacher to enter their login on a fake page.

Physical entry

Tries to get into buildings or server rooms to see if locks, guards, or cameras are strong enough.

Example: Following someone through a door (tailgating) to reach the computer lab without permission.

Security Testing

Purpose: Check how secure a system or network is. Examples include vulnerability scanning, ethical hacking, and penetration testing.

Types / Approaches:

Black-box testing

Tester knows nothing about the system.
Example: Trying to hack a company’s website from the Internet without any insider info.

White-box testing

Tester knows everything about the system.
Example: Checking all server configurations and code to find security flaws.

Gray-box / Internal testing

Tester knows some information, like an insider.
Example: An employee tries accessing restricted areas of the network to see what sensitive info they can reach.

Deliverables in Ethical Hacking

When an ethical hacker completes a security evaluation, the main deliverable is the Ethical Hacking Report. This report is the formal document that communicates the findings of the hacking activity to the organization. Its purpose is to clearly identify weaknesses in the system and provide actionable recommendations to improve security.

Contents of the Ethical Hacking Report

Results of the hacking activity:

The report compares what was planned in the work schedule (decided before starting the testing) with what was actually tested.
Example: If the plan was to test the company website and internal network, the report will show exactly which areas were tested and what the hacker found.

Details of vulnerabilities:

Every security gap, loophole, or weak point found is listed in detail.
Example: A webpage allowing unauthorized access or a password that is too easy to guess.

Suggestions and remediation:

The report provides advice on how to fix vulnerabilities or prevent attacks.
Example: Updating software, using stronger passwords, adding firewalls, or restricting access to sensitive areas.

Format: Usually delivered in hard copy, not digital form, to reduce the risk of sensitive data being leaked.

Other Important Considerations

Nondisclosure clause:

Only authorized personnel can access the report. The hacker cannot share findings outside the organization.
Example: The report is given to the IT manager, not posted online.

Integrity of the evaluation team:

The team must act honestly, professionally, and ethically while handling the testing and report.

Sensitivity of information:

The report often contains highly sensitive information about the organization’s systems.
Example: If the report mentions server vulnerabilities, it must be kept secure to prevent misuse.

Summary

The Ethical Hacking Report is a detailed, professional document that explains what was tested, what weaknesses were found, and how to fix them, while keeping all information confidential and secure.
