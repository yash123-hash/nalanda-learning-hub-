Module I — Introduction to Ethical Hacking

Can hacking be ethical? Terminology, elements of security and fundamentals.

Can Hacking Be Ethical?

The noun “hacker” describes someone who enjoys learning details of computer systems. Hacking can mean creative problem solving, reverse-engineering to improve software, or, when constrained by law and contract, assessing systems for security purposes.

Terminology

Cracker — often used for someone with offensive intent. Ethical hacker — security professional who uses skills defensively within legal boundaries.

  • Threat – potential adverse event.
  • Vulnerability – weakness leading to unexpected events.
  • Target of Evaluation – an IT system chosen for assessment.
  • Attack – intelligent action to violate security.
  • Exploit – method to breach a vulnerability.

Elements of Security

Security reduces the risk of theft, tampering, or disruption. Key elements:

  • Confidentiality — concealment of information.
  • Authenticity — assurance of origin/identity.
  • Integrity — protection against unauthorized modification.
  • Availability — access to resources when needed.

Phases of a Malicious (and Defensive) Hacker

Reconnaissance → Scanning → Gaining Access → Maintaining Access → Covering Tracks

Phase 1 - Reconnaissance

Gather public and semi-public information about a target. Passive reconnaissance is observation; active reconnaissance probes systems.

Passive: open-source intelligence, monitoring traffic. Active: probes to find hosts, ports and services.

Phase 2 - Scanning

Use scanners to find open ports, services and vulnerabilities. Tools include port scanners and vulnerability scanners.

Phase 3 - Gaining Access

Exploit vulnerabilities to obtain access. Examples: buffer overflows, SQLi, session hijacking. Business risk often high.

Phase 4 - Maintaining Access

Install persistence mechanisms (backdoors, trojans) if in-scope; otherwise document how persistence could be achieved.

Phase 5 - Covering Tracks

Techniques to avoid detection: log manipulation, tunneling, steganography. Ethical testers must follow removal procedures in contract.

What Do Ethical Hackers Do?

Ethical hackers test systems under written authorization and provide remediation guidance.

Module I Phases Overview

Ethical Hacker — Goals & Questions

When hired, ethical hackers ask: what is being protected, against whom, and what resources exist for protection? They measure exposure, impact, and detectability.

  • What can the intruder see? (Reconnaissance & Scanning)
  • What can an intruder do? (Gaining & Maintaining Access)
  • Does the target notice intrusions? (Detection & Covering Tracks)

Skill Profile

  • Strong computer fundamentals across OS, networks, web stacks.
  • Deep knowledge of Windows, Linux, Unix platforms.
  • Networking expertise: protocols, routing, switching and services.
  • Knowledge of security principles and incident response workflows.

Best Practices (Ethical & Legal)

  1. Have written authorization (scope, time, rules).
  2. Report findings clearly with remediation steps.
  3. Protect client data — avoid exfiltration or leaks.
  4. Coordinate with defenders to minimize operational impact.

Modes of Ethical Hacking

Remote network

An ethical hacker mimics an Internet attacker to test external-facing assets. Example: scanning websites and servers for vulnerabilities.

Remote dial-up network

Tests legacy phone/modem access for weak/default credentials or exposed services.

Local network

Simulates an insider or guest on the network attempting lateral movement or data access.

Stolen equipment

Assesses the impact of lost/stolen hardware to see whether data can be accessed without authorization.

Social engineering

Tests human behavior using phishing and pretexting to assess awareness and policy gaps.

Physical entry

Tests physical security (locks, guards, cameras) often by attempting tailgating or unauthorized access to server rooms.

Security Testing Approaches

Black-box testing

Tester knows nothing about the system — simulates an external attacker. Example: testing a public-facing website from the Internet.

White-box testing

Tester has full knowledge of systems (configs, source). Example: code review and full configuration checks.

Gray-box / Internal testing

Tester has partial knowledge (e.g., credentials or network maps). Example: an employee with restricted access attempting privilege escalation.

Deliverables in Ethical Hacking

What organizations receive after an assessment.

Ethical Hacking Report

Purpose: Document test scope, findings, evidence, impact and prioritized remediation steps.

Contents

  • Results — what was planned vs what was tested (areas, targets, exclusions).
  • Details of vulnerabilities — steps to reproduce and evidence.
  • Suggestions and remediation — prioritized, practical fixes.
  • Delivery format and handling — typically restricted access; protect sensitive data.

Other considerations

  • Nondisclosure: Only authorized personnel receive the report.
  • Integrity: Test teams must act professionally and ethically.
  • Sensitivity: Reports often contain critical technical details; protect them accordingly.
