Key Terms

Below is a list of important terms you should know as we progress through the lessons. Most definitions are adapted from NIST.

Bit

A binary digit having a value of 0 or 1. The atomic unit of information in computing.

Byte

A group of eight bits that is treated either as a single entity or as an array of 8 individual bits.

Software

Computer programs and associated data that can be dynamically written and modified during execution.

Network

A system implemented with a collection of interconnected components such as routers, hubs, cabling, and controllers.

Defense-in-Depth

An information security strategy that integrates people, technology, and operations to establish layered barriers.

Authentication

Verifying a user is who they say they are.

Authorization

Validating that a claimed identity is known to the system and verifying what that user may access.

Identification

Asserting an identity and having it confirmed.

Confidentiality

Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure.

Integrity

Guarding against improper modification or destruction; includes non-repudiation and authenticity.

Availability

Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information.

Non-Repudiation

Evidence that prevents denial of actions — e.g., proof of who sent a message so the sender cannot deny it.

Vulnerability

A weakness or flaw that could permit undesirable actions on assets.

Threat

A source of harm that acts upon (exploits) a vulnerability.

Risk

The possibility of a negative event occurring when a threat acts upon a vulnerability.

Threat Actor

An individual or group that exploits vulnerabilities in technology.

CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is a foundational model in cybersecurity that represents the three main goals of information security:

🛡️ Confidentiality

Ensures that information is accessible only to authorized users and not disclosed to unauthorized individuals.

Example: Using passwords or encryption to protect data.

⚙️ Integrity

Ensures that information is accurate, complete, and unaltered during storage or transmission.

Example: Using checksums or hashing to detect unauthorized changes.

🔄 Availability

Ensures that information and resources are accessible to authorized users whenever needed.

Example: Using backups, redundant systems, and DDoS protection to maintain uptime.

👉 In Short

Confidentiality = Keep it secret

Integrity = Keep it correct

Availability = Keep it accessible

Parts of a Network

A brief, responsive reference describing common devices and endpoints you'll find on a typical network.

What is a Network?

In simple terms, a network is two or more computers linked together to share resources, files and services. Home networks commonly include desktops, laptops, phones, tablets and often smart devices (TVs, doorbells, thermostats, assistants).

Router

A router connects networks and routes traffic between them — for example, connecting your home LAN to the internet. Routers decide the best path for data to travel from source to destination.

Switch

A switch connects nodes on the same network (LAN). Unlike a hub, a switch forwards data only to the intended recipient using MAC-address learning, so it is more efficient and secure than old hubs.

Hub

A hub broadcasts data to every connected node and cannot target a single destination. Hubs are largely obsolete but useful as a comparison point when learning networking fundamentals.

Endpoints

Endpoints are devices that users interact with directly — desktops, laptops, phones, tablets, printers, etc. They are where data typically ends up for human consumption.

Servers

Servers provide dedicated services to the network, such as web servers, email servers, and print servers. They serve resources to endpoints and other devices.

Firewall

Firewalls filter traffic going between networks to protect against threats from the internet (spam, viruses, and unauthorized access). They enforce network security policies.

Quick Review of Terms

  • Hub — broadcasts to every device connected to it.
  • Switch — connects multiple nodes on the same network and forwards traffic to specific nodes.
  • Router — connects networks and routes traffic between them.
  • Endpoint — end-user device like a desktop, laptop or phone.
  • Server — a computer providing a dedicated service (mail, print, web).
  • Firewall — filters and protects traffic between networks.
