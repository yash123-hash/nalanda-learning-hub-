Everything is Online

“Hey Shad, are you online?” “Of course, I am!” We expect our devices — phones, tablets, laptops, and desktops — to always be connected to the global internet. We use this network to interact with friends, shop, share pictures, and learn. The internet has become such a part of everyday life that we almost take it for granted.

Normally, when people use the term internet, they think of it as a formless collection of connections — a “place” people go to find or share information. The internet is a worldwide collection of interconnected networks cooperating to exchange information using common standards.

Who Owns “The Internet”?

The internet is not owned by any individual or group. It is a network of networks. Everything you access online is hosted somewhere on that global fabric — social media, games, email, online courses — each connected to local networks that send and receive information.

Think about interactions you have daily that require you to be online. Examples:

  • Intelligent networks deliver news and email to handheld devices.
  • Video conferencing connects people around the globe instantly.
  • Phones share voice, text, and images globally.
  • Online gaming connects thousands of players seamlessly.

Local Networks

Local networks range from two computers to hundreds of thousands of devices. SOHO (Small Office/Home Office) networks let you share printers, documents, and media between a few local users. Larger networks provide business services such as advertising, ordering supplies, and customer communications.

Small Home Networks

Small home networks connect a few computers to each other and to the internet.

Medium to Large Networks & World Wide Networks

Medium to large networks can span many locations with thousands of hosts. The internet as a whole connects hundreds of millions of computers worldwide — a true network of networks.

Mobile Devices

Smartphone

Smartphones connect to the internet from almost anywhere. They combine phone, camera, GPS, media player and a small computer in one device.

Tablet

Tablets offer larger screens for videos and reading. Many tasks once reserved for laptops can be performed on tablets with on-screen keyboards.

Smartwatch

Smartwatches connect to smartphones to show alerts, heart rate, steps, and other health info.

Smart Glasses

Wearable displays (like Google Glass) provide small HUD-style screens and touchpads for hands-free navigation.

Connected Home Devices

Security System

Monitored and configured remotely using mobile apps.

Appliances

Refrigerators, ovens and other appliances can be monitored or controlled remotely.

Smart TV

Smart TVs connect to the internet for streaming and browsing without extra set-top boxes.

Gaming Console

Consoles connect online for downloads and multiplayer gaming.

Cars can connect to networks for navigation, alerts, diagnostics and telematics.

Used to track objects or monitor sensors.

Pacemakers and monitors often provide alerts and telemetry to medical staff.

Understanding Data & Its Types

Data is the information you input, transmit, or store. Types include volunteer data (you knowingly share it), inferred data (generated from your actions), and observed data (collected automatically, e.g., location).

Bandwidth

Bandwidth is the maximum amount of data that can be transmitted over a channel in a given time (measured in bps, Kbps, Mbps, Gbps).

Bandwidth is the capacity (speed limit); throughput is the actual data rate achieved. Many factors (latency, packet loss, congestion) reduce throughput.

Throughput

Throughput is the actual amount of data successfully transmitted over time. Example: a 100 Mbps link may only deliver 70 Mbps throughput due to overhead or congestion.

  • Causes of reduced throughput: congestion, latency, packet loss, protocol overhead, hardware limits.

Clients & Servers in Modern Networks

Hosts can act as clients, servers, or both. Server software provides services (web, email). Client software (browsers, mail clients) requests and displays those services.

Client and Server Roles & Peer-to-Peer Networks

Hosts that run both client and server software form peer-to-peer networks (P2P). P2P is easy to set up and low cost but less secure and not as scalable as dedicated-server models.

Advantages of P2P

  • Easy to set up
  • Less complex
  • Lower cost

Disadvantages of P2P

  • No centralized administration
  • Not as secure
  • Not scalable

Example: Instant messaging where both clients can send and receive simultaneously.

Network Infrastructure

The path a message takes may be a single cable or a global network. The infrastructure includes end devices, intermediate devices, and network media.

End devices

  • Desktop, Laptop, Printer, IP Phone, Tablet, TelePresence endpoint

Intermediate devices

  • Wireless Router, LAN Switch, Router, Multilayer Switch, Firewall

Network media

  • Wireless media, LAN media, WAN media

An end device (host) is the source or destination of messages. Addresses uniquely identify hosts on the network.

ISP Services

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) connects your home network to the wider internet. ISPs may also offer email, storage, hosting, backups and security services.

ISP Connections

The internet backbone is a web of fiber-optic cables and high-speed equipment. Home connections (cable, DSL, cellular, satellite) link users to this backbone.

Cable & DSL

Cable: Internet carried on coaxial cable (same as cable TV). Requires a cable modem.

DSL: Runs over phone lines and uses a DSL modem. DSL has separate channels for voice, download and upload.

Other Options

  • Cellular: Uses mobile network; convenient for mobility and areas without wired connectivity.
  • Satellite: Useful where wired options are unavailable; requires a clear line of sight.
  • Dial-up: Low bandwidth, useful only when higher-speed options are unavailable.

Wireless Networks

Phones and devices contain multiple radios: cellular, Wi‑Fi, Bluetooth, NFC and GPS. These operate simultaneously for different functions (voice, data, location, short-range pairing).

Wi‑Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, GPS

Wi‑Fi

Connects devices to local networks and the internet. Range depends on access point power and environment.

Bluetooth

Low-power, short-range wireless for accessories (headsets, keyboards). Supports multiple simultaneous connections.

NFC

Very short-range communication used for payments and quick pairing.

GPS

Receives satellite signals to determine device location.

Mobile Devices and Wi‑Fi

Mobile devices enable productivity and learning anywhere. Use Wi‑Fi when possible to save cellular data and battery life; secure Wi‑Fi with WPA2/WPA3 and VPNs for sensitive data.

Wi‑Fi Settings (Quick)

Android — Turn Wi‑Fi On/Off

  1. Settings → Network & Internet → Wi‑Fi → toggle ON
  2. Select network, enter password, Connect

Manual Connect

  1. Settings → Add network → enter SSID, security and password → Save

iOS — Turn Wi‑Fi On/Off

  1. Settings → Wi‑Fi → toggle ON
  2. Choose a network, enter password, Join

Manual Connect

  1. Settings → Wi‑Fi → Other → enter SSID and security → Join

How Phones Choose Networks

  • Wi‑Fi first (when available)
  • Cellular second (5G/4G/3G)
  • Transitions are automatic and seamless

Bluetooth Pairing (Example)

Home Network Basics

Components of a Home Network

Home networks connect many devices: PCs, consoles, smart TVs, printers, cameras and IoT devices. Routers provide wired LAN ports, wireless AP, and the WAN port to the ISP.

A Home WLAN

A Wireless LAN (WLAN) uses Wi‑Fi to connect devices within the home without cables. Wired and wireless devices typically are on the same local network.

Typical Home Routers

Routers combine routing, switching and wireless access. LAN (Ethernet) ports connect wired devices; the WAN port connects to the ISP modem.

Quick summary: Wired devices (Ethernet) and wireless devices (Wi‑Fi) are on the same local network. The WAN port connects your router to the internet.

Network Technologies in the Home

LAN Wireless Frequencies

Common Wi‑Fi bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. Bluetooth also uses 2.4 GHz for short-range connections. Wi‑Fi typically provides greater range and throughput than Bluetooth.

Wired Network Technologies

Ethernet (UTP with RJ‑45) remains the preferred wired option for speed and stability. Powerline adapters can use electrical wiring where Ethernet isn't available.

Category 5e, Coaxial & Fiber

  • Cat5e: Twisted-pair cable for reliable LAN connections.
  • Coaxial: Used for cable TV and some internet modems.
  • Fiber-optic: High-speed backbone and last-mile broadband in many areas.
