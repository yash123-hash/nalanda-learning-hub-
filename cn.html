Everything is Online

We expect phones, tablets, laptops and desktops to be connected to the internet. The internet is a global network of networks — not owned by any single person or group, but made up of many interconnected systems.

Who Owns “The Internet”?

No single owner. The internet is a network of networks: services you access are hosted on many different servers that interconnect through ISPs and backbone providers.

Examples of daily interactions that require being online:

  • News and email delivered to handheld devices.
  • Video conferencing connecting people across the globe.
  • Phones sharing voice, text and images worldwide.
  • Online gaming connecting thousands of players in real time.

Local Networks

Local networks range from two computers to tens of thousands of devices. SOHO (Small Office/Home Office) networks let you share printers, files, and media among a few users.

Small Home Networks

Small home networks typically connect a few computers and devices to each other and to the internet through a router and modem.

Medium to Large Networks & World Wide Networks

Medium and large networks span multiple locations with many hosts. The internet connects hundreds of millions of devices — a true network of networks.

Mobile Devices

Smartphone

Smartphones connect to the internet from almost anywhere, combining phone, camera, GPS, media player and a small computer in one device.

Tablet

Tablets offer larger screens for videos and reading. Many tasks once reserved for laptops can now be performed on tablets.

Smartwatch

Smartwatches connect to smartphones to show alerts, health data and quick interactions.

Smart Glasses

Wearable displays provide small HUD-style screens and hands-free navigation for specific use-cases.

Understanding Data & Its Types

Data can be volunteered by you, inferred from your actions, or observed and collected automatically. Understanding data types helps you design and secure networks appropriately.

Bandwidth

Bandwidth is the maximum amount of data that can be transmitted over a channel in a given time (bps, Kbps, Mbps, Gbps).

Bandwidth is like a capacity (speed limit); throughput is the actual data rate you achieve. Latency, packet loss and congestion often reduce throughput.

Throughput

Throughput is the actual amount of data successfully transmitted over time. For example, a 100 Mbps link may only deliver 70 Mbps throughput due to overhead or congestion.

  • Typical causes of reduced throughput: congestion, latency, packet loss, protocol overhead and hardware limits.

Clients & Servers in Modern Networks

Hosts can act as clients, servers, or both. Server software provides services like web and email, while client software (browsers, mail clients) requests those services.

Client and Server Roles & Peer-to-Peer Networks

Hosts that run both client and server software form peer-to-peer networks (P2P). P2P is easy to set up and inexpensive but can be less secure and less scalable than dedicated servers.

Advantages of P2P

  • Easy to set up
  • Less complex
  • Lower cost

Disadvantages of P2P

  • No centralized administration
  • Not as secure
  • Not scalable

Example: Instant messaging where both clients can send and receive simultaneously.

Network Infrastructure

Messages travel over paths that might be local cables or the global internet. Infrastructure includes end devices, intermediate devices, and network media.

End devices

  • Desktop, Laptop, Printer, IP Phone, Tablet, TelePresence endpoint

Intermediate devices

  • Wireless Router, LAN Switch, Router, Multilayer Switch, Firewall

Network media

  • Wireless media, LAN media, WAN media

An end device (host) is the source or destination of messages. Addresses uniquely identify hosts on the network.

ISP Services

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) connects your home network to the wider internet. ISPs may also offer email, storage, hosting, backups and security services.

ISP Connections

The internet backbone is a web of fiber-optic cables and high-speed equipment. Home connections (cable, DSL, cellular, satellite) link users to this backbone.

Cable & DSL

Cable: Internet carried on coaxial cable (same as cable TV). Requires a cable modem.

DSL: Runs over phone lines and uses a DSL modem. DSL can separate voice, download and upload channels.

Other Options

  • Cellular: Uses mobile network; convenient for mobility and areas without wired connectivity.
  • Satellite: Useful where wired options are unavailable; requires a clear line of sight.
  • Dial-up: Low bandwidth, useful only when higher-speed options are unavailable.

Wireless Networks

Most mobile devices include multiple radios: cellular, Wi‑Fi, Bluetooth, NFC and GPS. They can operate concurrently for voice, data, location and short-range pairing.

Wi‑Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, GPS

Wi‑Fi

Connects devices to local networks and the internet. Range depends on access point power and environment.

Bluetooth

Low-power, short-range wireless for accessories (headsets, keyboards). Supports multiple simultaneous connections.

NFC

Very short-range communication used for payments and quick pairing.

GPS

Receives satellite signals to determine device location.

Mobile Devices and Wi‑Fi

Mobile devices enable productivity anywhere. Use Wi‑Fi when possible to save cellular data and battery — secure Wi‑Fi with WPA2/WPA3 and VPNs for sensitive data.

Wi‑Fi Settings (Quick)

Android — Turn Wi‑Fi On/Off

  1. Settings → Network & Internet → Wi‑Fi → toggle ON
  2. Select network, enter password, Connect
  3. For manual networks: Settings → Add network → enter SSID, security and password → Save

iOS — Turn Wi‑Fi On/Off

  1. Settings → Wi‑Fi → toggle ON
  2. Select network, enter password, Connect
  3. To add network: Settings → Wi‑Fi → Other → enter SSID and credentials → Join

How Phones Choose Networks

  • Wi‑Fi first (when available)
  • Cellular second (5G/4G/3G)
  • Transitions are automatic and seamless when supported

Bluetooth Pairing (Example)

  1. Put the accessory in discoverable mode
  2. Settings → Bluetooth → Scan → select device
  3. Enter PIN if required and confirm pairing

Home Network Basics

Components of a Home Network

Home networks connect many devices: PCs, consoles, smart TVs, printers, cameras and IoT devices. Routers provide LAN ports, wireless access, and the WAN port to the ISP.

A Home WLAN

A Wireless LAN (WLAN) uses Wi‑Fi to connect devices in the home. Wired and wireless devices usually share the same local network.

Typical Home Routers

Routers combine routing, switching and wireless access. LAN ports connect wired devices; the WAN port connects your router to the ISP modem.

Quick summary: Wired devices (Ethernet) and wireless devices (Wi‑Fi) are on the same local network. The WAN port connects your router to the internet.

Network Technologies in the Home

LAN Wireless Frequencies

Common Wi‑Fi bands: 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. Bluetooth also uses 2.4 GHz for short-range connections. Wi‑Fi typically provides greater range and throughput than Bluetooth.

Wired Network Technologies

Ethernet (UTP with RJ‑45) remains the preferred wired option for speed and stability. Powerline adapters can use electrical wiring where Ethernet isn't available.

Category 5e, Coaxial & Fiber

  • Cat5e: Twisted-pair cable for reliable LAN connections.
  • Coaxial: Used for cable TV and some internet modems.
  • Fiber-optic: High-speed backbone and last-mile broadband.
