Everything is Online

“Hey Shad, are you online?” “Of course, I am!” How many of us still think about whether or not we are “online”? We expect our devices, cell phones, tablets, laptops and desktop computers to always be connected to the global internet. We use this network to interact with our friends, shop, share pictures and experiences, and learn. The internet has become such a part of everyday life that we almost take it for granted.

Normally, when people use the term internet, they are not referring to the physical connections in the real world. Rather, they tend to think of it as a formless collection of connections. It is the “place” people go to find or share information.

Who Owns “The Internet”?

The internet is not owned by any individual or group. The internet is a worldwide collection of interconnected networks (internetwork or internet for short), cooperating with each other to exchange information using common standards. Through telephone wires, fiber-optic cables, wireless transmissions, and satellite links, internet users can exchange information in a variety of forms, as shown in the figure.

Everything that you access online is located somewhere on the global internet. Social media sites, multiplayer games, messaging centers that provide email, online courses — all of these internet destinations are connected to local networks that send and receive information through the internet.

Think about all of the interactions that you have during the day which require you to be online.

examples:-
  • Intelligent networks allow handheld devices to receive news and emails, and to send text.
  • Video conferencing instantly connects people around the globe.
  • Phones connect globally to share voice, text, and images.
  • Online gaming connects thousands of people seamlessly.

Local Networks

Local networks come in all sizes. They can range from simple networks consisting of two computers, to networks connecting hundreds of thousands of devices. Networks installed in small offices, or homes and home offices, are referred to as small office/home office (SOHO) networks. SOHO networks let you share resources such as printers, documents, pictures, and music, between a few local users.

In business, large networks can be used to advertise and sell products, order supplies, and communicate with customers. Communication over a network is usually more efficient and less expensive than traditional forms of communication, such as regular mail or long distance phone calls. Networks allow for rapid communication such as email and instant messaging, and provide consolidation and access to information stored on network servers.

Business and SOHO networks usually provide a shared connection to the internet. The internet is considered a "network of networks" because it is literally made up of thousands of local networks that are connected to each other.

Understanding Data & Its Types

We hear about data all the time, especially when we're talking about computer networks.

You hear about data that's transmitted, data that's entered, data that's stored, and you even hear about big data that exists somewhere out in the cloud, but what exactly is data?

Okay, data in its raw form is the information that you input. Every time you hit send or share or upload, you are sending data that you created somewhere out onto the network.

Now, there are different types of data. I'm going to talk a little bit first about volunteer data.

Volunteer data is data that you offer yourself. You realize the data is being collected about you, and you are agreeing to share it or to store it somewhere on the network.

The second type of data that you may not realize is actually being collected about you is something we call inferred data. Inferred data is data that you generate by your activities.

For example, your credit card. Every place that you use your credit card keeps a record, and therefore, if you move about and go to different stores, things are being collected about you, like your preferences, the places that you typically eat or shop, and also, information that helps pinpoint your location.

For example, I'm here in Portland today, and I used my ATM card. Now, I live in Florida. So my bank has now data that indicates that I traveled from Florida to Portland, and that information then becomes part of a larger databank that is stored at the bank and also at the credit card processing company. So this data exists, even though I didn't intentionally volunteer it.

Now, another type of data is observed data. For example, your phone probably keeps track of your location. Most people have their location services on. That information is also being stored and is being transmitted to your carrier.

So you have a lot of information out there that is being collected and stored—hence, the term big data out in the cloud. So we have to be careful sometimes when we click on the share or store buttons on our devices so that we know where things are going.

Bandwidth

In Computer Networks, Bandwidth is the maximum amount of data that can be transmitted over a communication channel (like a cable, wireless link, or fiber optic) in a given amount of time.

  • Measured in: bits per second (bps) or multiples (Kbps, Mbps, Gbps, Tbps).
  • Represents: The capacity of a network link, not the actual speed you always get.
  • If a network link has 100 Mbps bandwidth, it means at most 100 Megabits of data can be transmitted per second.
  • Actual speed might be less due to latency, packet loss, congestion, or protocol overhead.

Types:

  1. Digital Bandwidth – data rate in bits per second.
  2. Analog Bandwidth – range of frequencies a channel can carry, measured in Hertz (Hz).

Affects data transfer rate (downloads, streaming, VoIP). Determines network performance in LAN, WAN, or Internet. Plays a role in Quality of Service (QoS) for video calls, gaming, etc.

Throughput

Throughput in computer networks refers to the actual amount of data successfully transmitted from one point to another in a given period of time.

Measured in: bits per second (bps), kilobits per second (Kbps), megabits per second (Mbps), or gigabits per second (Gbps).

Relationship: Bandwidth = maximum theoretical data transfer capacity of a network link. Throughput = actual data transfer rate achieved in practice.

Example: A link with 100 Mbps bandwidth may only achieve 70 Mbps throughput due to delays, congestion, or errors.

Common causes that reduce throughput:

  • Network congestion – too many users transmitting data simultaneously.
  • Latency – delays caused by distance or processing.
  • Packet loss – data needing retransmission.
  • Protocol overhead – headers, acknowledgments, etc.
  • Hardware limitations – router, switch, or end device capacity.

Summary: Bandwidth is the speed limit, Throughput is the actual speed you get.

Clients & Servers in Modern Networks

In modern networks, computer hosts can act as a client, a server, or both. The software installed on the computer determines which role the computer plays.

Servers

Servers are hosts that have software installed which enable them to provide information to other hosts on the network, like email or webpages.

Each server service requires a separate server software.

Clients

Clients are computer hosts that have software installed that enables the host to request and display the information obtained from the server.

An example of client software is a web browser such as Chrome, Edge, Safari, or Firefox. Using web browser software, the client can request the webpage from the server. The web server will respond with the webpage.

Another example is email. Clients use an email client software such as Microsoft Outlook to access email on the server. Clients can both send and receive email messages from the email server.

Client and Server Roles & Peer-to-Peer Networks

Client and Server Roles

All computers connected to a network that participate directly in network communication are classified as hosts. Hosts can send and receive messages on the network. In modern networks, computer hosts can act as a client, a server, or both. The software installed on the computer determines which role the computer plays.

Servers are hosts that have software installed which enable them to provide information, like email or web pages, to other hosts on the network. Each service requires separate server software. For example, a host requires web server software in order to provide web services to the network. Every destination that you visit online is provided to you by a server located somewhere on a network that is connected to the global internet.

Clients are computer hosts that have software installed that enables the hosts to request and display the information obtained from the server. An example of client software is a web browser, such as Internet Explorer, Safari, Mozilla Firefox, or Chrome.

Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Networks

Client and server software usually run on separate computers, but it is also possible for one computer to run both client and server software at the same time. In small businesses and homes, many computers function as the servers and clients on the network. This type of network is called a peer-to-peer (P2P) network.

The simplest P2P network consists of two directly connected computers using either a wired or wireless connection. Both computers are then able to use this simple network to exchange data and services with each other, acting as either a client or a server as necessary.

Multiple PCs can also be connected to create a larger P2P network, but this requires a network device, such as a switch, to interconnect the computers.

The main disadvantage of a P2P environment is that the performance of a host can be slowed down if it is acting as both a client and a server at the same time. In larger businesses, because of the potential for high amounts of network traffic, it is often necessary to have dedicated servers to support the number of service requests.

Advantages of P2P

  • Easy to set up
  • Less complex
  • Lower cost because network devices and dedicated servers may not be required
  • Can be used for simple tasks such as transferring files and sharing printers

Disadvantages of P2P

  • No centralized administration
  • Not as secure
  • Not scalable
  • All devices may act as both clients and servers which can slow their performance

Peer-to-Peer Applications

A P2P application allows a device to act as both a client and a server within the same communication. In this model, every client is a server and every server is a client. P2P applications require that each end device provide a user interface and run a background service.

Some P2P applications use a hybrid system where resource sharing is decentralized, but the indexes that point to resource locations are stored in a centralized directory. In a hybrid system, each peer accesses an index server to get the location of a resource stored on another peer.

Example: Instant messaging where both clients can simultaneously send and receive messages.

Multiple Roles in the Network

A computer with server software can provide services simultaneously to one or many clients. Additionally, a single computer can run multiple types of server software. In a home or small business, it may be necessary for one computer to act as a file server, a web server, and an email server.

A single computer can also run multiple types of client software. There must be client software for every service required. With multiple clients installed, a host can connect to multiple servers at the same time. For example, a user can check email and view a web page while instant messaging and listening to internet radio.

End Devices

The network devices that people are most familiar with are called end devices, or hosts. These devices form the interface between users and the underlying communication network.

Some examples of end devices are as follows:

  • Computers (workstations, laptops, file servers, web servers)
  • Network printers
  • Telephones and teleconferencing equipment
  • Security cameras
  • Mobile devices (such as smart phones, tablets, PDAs, and wireless debit/credit card readers and barcode scanners)

An end device (or host) is either the source or destination of a message transmitted over the network. In order to uniquely identify hosts, addresses are used. When a host initiates communication, it uses the address of the destination host to specify where the message should be sent.

ISP Services

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) provides the link between the home network and the internet. An ISP can be the local cable provider, a landline telephone service provider, the cellular network that provides your smartphone service, or an independent provider who leases bandwidth on the physical network infrastructure of another company.

Many ISPs also offer additional services to their contract subscribers, as shown in the figure. These services can include email accounts, network storage, and website hosting and automated backup or security services.

ISPs are critical to communications across the global internet. Each ISP connects to other ISPs to form a network of links that interconnect users all over the world. ISPs are connected in a hierarchical manner that ensures that internet traffic generally takes the shortest path from the source to the destination.

The internet backbone is like an information super highway that provides high-speed data links to connect the various service provider networks in major metropolitan areas around the world. The primary medium that connects the internet backbone is fiber-optic cable. This cable is typically installed underground to connect cities within continents. Fiber-optic cables also run under the sea to connect continents, countries, and cities.

ISP Connections

The interconnection of ISPs that forms the backbone of the internet is a complex web of fiber-optic cables with expensive networking switches and routers that direct the flow of information between source and destination hosts. Average home users are not aware of the infrastructure outside of their network. For a home user, connecting to the ISP is a fairly uncomplicated process.

Cable and DSL Connections

Cable

Typically offered by cable television service providers, the internet data signal is carried on the same coaxial cable that delivers cable television. It provides a high bandwidth, always-on connection to the internet. A special cable modem separates the internet data signal from the other signals carried on the cable and provides an Ethernet connection to a host computer or LAN.

DSL (Digital Subscriber Line)

DSL provides a high bandwidth, always-on connection to the internet. It requires a special high-speed modem that separates the DSL signal from the telephone signal and provides an Ethernet connection to a host computer or LAN. DSL runs over a telephone line, with the line split into three channels:

  • One channel is used for voice telephone calls (you can receive calls without disconnecting from the internet).
  • A faster download channel, used to receive information from the internet.
  • An upload channel for sending information (usually slightly slower than the download channel).

The quality and speed of the DSL connection depend mainly on the quality of the phone line and the distance from the central office of your phone company. The farther you are from the central office, the slower the connection.

Additional Connectivity Options

Cellular

Cellular internet access uses a cell phone network to connect. Wherever you can get a cellular signal, you can get internet access. Performance is limited by the capabilities of the phone and the connected cell tower. It’s a real benefit in areas without wired connectivity or for people on the move, but carriers usually meter bandwidth and may charge extra for usage beyond the data plan.

Satellite

Satellite service is a good option for homes or offices that do not have access to DSL or cable. Dishes require a clear line of sight to the satellite, which can be difficult in heavily wooded areas or where there are overhead obstructions. Speeds vary by contract, equipment and installation costs can be high (sometimes offset by provider deals), followed by a moderate monthly fee. Like cellular, it’s especially useful in otherwise unconnected areas.

Dial-Up Telephone

An inexpensive option that uses any phone line and a modem. To connect, a user dials the ISP’s access number. The low bandwidth is usually insufficient for large data transfers, but it can be useful for basic, temporary access while traveling. Consider dial-up only when higher-speed options are unavailable.

Other Wireless Networks

Global Positioning System

The GPS uses satellites to transmit signals that cover the globe. The smartphone can receive these signals and calculate the phone’s location to an accuracy of within 10 meters.

Wi-Fi

Wi-Fi transmitters and receivers located within the smartphone enable the phone to connect to local networks and the internet. Bluetooth

Bluetooth is a low-power, shorter range wireless technology that is intended to replace wired connectivity for accessories such as speakers, headphones, and microphones. Near Field Communication (NFC)

Near Field Communication (NFC) is a wireless communication technology that enables data to be exchanged by devices that are in very close proximity to each other, usually less than a few centimeters. For example, NFC can be used to connect a smartphone and a payment system. NFC uses electromagnetic fields to transmit data.

Mobile Devices and Wi‑Fi

Mobile devices give us the freedom to work, learn, play, and communicate wherever we want. People using mobile devices do not need to be tied to a physical location to send and receive voice, video, and data communications. Like other Wi‑Fi‑enabled devices, it is important to use security when connecting to Wi‑Fi networks. These precautions should be taken to protect Wi‑Fi communications on mobile devices:

  • Never send login or password information using unencrypted text (plaintext).
  • Use a VPN connection when possible if you are sending sensitive data.
  • Enable security on home networks.
  • Use WPA2 or higher encryption for security.

Home Network Basics

Components of a Home Network

A home network today connects far more than just an integrated router. Many different devices rely on it for connectivity and smooth functioning. For example, desktop computers connect for work, study, and browsing, while gaming systems such as PlayStation or Xbox use the network for online multiplayer gaming. Smart TVs depend on the network to stream movies, shows, and music. Devices like printers and scanners use it for wireless printing and file sharing. Security cameras stream real-time video for home monitoring, and telephones using VoIP services connect calls over the internet. Even climate control devices like smart thermostats rely on the network to adjust room temperatures remotely. With the arrival of new technologies such as smart lights, appliances, and voice assistants, more and more household functions will continue to depend on the home network for connectivity and control.

A Home Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN)

A Home Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) is a type of network that allows devices in a home to connect to the internet and to each other without physical cables, using Wi‑Fi technology. Instead of plugging devices directly into the router with wires, a WLAN uses radio signals to provide connectivity within a certain range, usually covering the entire house.

For example, when you connect your smartphone to your home Wi‑Fi, it’s using the WLAN. Other devices such as laptops, gaming consoles, smart TVs, printers, security cameras, and smart home devices (like Alexa or Google Nest) also connect to this same WLAN. This makes it easy for family members to access the internet from anywhere in the home and also share resources, like printing from a wireless printer or streaming video on a TV.

👉 In short, a Home WLAN creates a wireless network environment inside the home, enabling convenience, mobility, and support for multiple connected devices.

Typical Home Network Routers

A typical home network router is the central device that connects all household devices to the internet and to each other. It usually combines multiple functions into a single unit to make networking simple for home users.

Ethernet Port

In a typical home router, there are several ports that connect to its internal switch. These ports are usually labeled “Ethernet” or “LAN” and are used to connect wired devices directly to the router using Ethernet cables. When devices such as desktop computers, gaming consoles, smart TVs, or printers are plugged into these LAN ports, they all become part of the same local network. This means they can communicate with each other easily—for example, a computer can send a document to a printer, or a gaming console can access files from a shared computer—while also sharing the router’s internet connection.

👉 Example: If you plug your desktop computer and smart TV into the router’s LAN ports, both devices will be on the same home network, allowing the TV to stream videos from the computer’s media library.

Internet (WAN) Port

A home router has a special port called the Internet port or WAN (Wide Area Network) port, which is different from the LAN (Ethernet) ports. LAN ports are used to connect devices within the home network, such as computers, printers, or gaming consoles, so they can share files and resources locally. In contrast, the WAN port connects the router to another network, usually the internet, through a cable modem or DSL modem provided by the Internet Service Provider (ISP). Once connected, the router distributes internet access to all devices in the home, whether they are connected by Ethernet cables (LAN) or wirelessly through Wi‑Fi.

👉 Example: Your LAN ports may connect a desktop computer, printer, and smart TV, while the WAN port links the router to the ISP’s modem, giving every device internet access.

Quick Summary

  • Home Routers: Have both wired LAN ports and a wireless access point (Wi‑Fi).
  • Radio Antenna: Provides wireless connectivity for devices like laptops, phones, smart TVs.
  • Same Local Network:
    • Devices on LAN ports (wired)
    • Devices on Wi‑Fi (wireless)
    → Both are on the same local network by default.
  • Internet/WAN Port: The only port on a different network → connects router to ISP’s modem for internet.

👉 Example: A desktop (LAN cable) and a smartphone (Wi‑Fi) are on the same home network, while the WAN port links the router to the internet.

Network Technologies in the Home

LAN Wireless Frequencies

In home networks, the most common wireless technologies operate in the unlicensed 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz frequency ranges. One such technology is Bluetooth, which uses the 2.4 GHz band for low-speed, short-range communication. Its strength lies in its ability to support one-to-many connections, making it ideal for connecting multiple peripherals such as wireless mice, keyboards, and printers. Bluetooth is also widely used for transmitting audio to speakers and headphones. In contrast, modern wireless LAN technologies based on the IEEE 802.11 standards (Wi-Fi) also operate in the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands but transmit at much higher power levels, providing greater range and faster data throughput. These Wi-Fi connections are essential for devices like smartphones, laptops, and smart TVs to access the internet across the home. Both Bluetooth and Wi-Fi operate in frequency ranges that can be used without a special license or permit, which has helped them become the dominant wireless technologies in households.

👉 Example: A Bluetooth headset uses 2.4 GHz for short-range audio, while a Wi-Fi router uses 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz to connect multiple devices throughout the home.

Wired Network Technologies

Some home network devices still benefit from a wired connection, even though most support wireless communication. Wired connections are generally faster, more stable, and not shared with other users on the network, making them ideal for applications like gaming, streaming, or desktop computing. The most common wired technology is the Ethernet protocol, which enables communication over a wired LAN. Ethernet usually uses patch cables made of unshielded twisted pair (UTP) with RJ-45 connectors, available in various lengths. Many modern homes are built with Ethernet wall jacks for easy wired connectivity. In homes without built-in Ethernet wiring, powerline adapters can be used to provide network access through the existing electrical wiring.

👉 Example: A gaming console or smart TV connected via Ethernet often performs better than one relying on Wi-Fi.

Category 5e cable

The most common wiring in LANs is Category 5e (Cat5e) cable, which contains four pairs of twisted wires. The twisting reduces electrical interference, making the cable reliable and suitable for fast, stable network connections in homes and offices.

👉 Example: A Cat5e cable is often used to connect a computer to a router for smooth internet access.

Coaxial cable

A coaxial cable has an inner wire, an insulating layer, a conducting shield, and an outer jacket for protection. It is durable, resists interference, and is commonly used for TV, internet, and CCTV connections.

👉 Example: The wire connecting a cable TV box or modem is usually coaxial.

Fiber-optic cables

Fiber-optic cables are made of glass or plastic strands, about the thickness of a human hair, and are capable of carrying digital information at very high speeds over long distances. They offer extremely high bandwidth, allowing the transfer of large amounts of data quickly and efficiently.

👉 Example: Fiber-optic cables are used in broadband internet connections to deliver fast speeds and support heavy data usage like streaming and online gaming.
